A 30 Day Challenge

School work has dismantled all the plans I once had to religiously practice D3.js. Data visualization has been an obsession of mine for the last two years, but writing and, before that, research, have scuttled my plans to dive deeply into the topic. This winter, while sitting at home in my New York apartment, I've decided to try, again, to make a concerted effort to practice D3.js on a daily basis. That practice is being done in (daily) coordination with Python and web scraping learning; the goal is to become really well-versed in Python for data cleaning, web scraping, and then D3.js for visualization.

Every day, for the next 30 days, I will create a chart with D3.js. Each day's post will grow in complexity; annotations will emerge, as will more complex color scales and chart types. All visualizations are made with D3.js, and many of the visuals are adapted directly from Amelia Wattenberger's excellent book on the topic.

I wrote my first code in D3 maybe 7 or 8 months ago, but my progress has been off and on. Throughout this time, I have always been aware of Observable.com, but I never really understood how it works. Observable is kind of like a Jupyter Notebook for JavaScript, but I had trouble understanding its quirks. Still, many of the programmers that I follow on Twitter (like Shirley Wu and Amelia Wattenberger) have published notebooks on Observable in the past. I decided, finally, to overcome my apprehension, fight through the Observable learning curve, and begin to build data visualizations on that website. I think it might be a useful tool for my coding, and there are LOADS of great data visualization examples on there.

I decided to start creating my data visualizations on Observable; it seems like a great way to learn both D3.js and become familiar with the quirks of that website simultaneously. View my chart from every day of this challenge by clicking on the button below. Onwards!
